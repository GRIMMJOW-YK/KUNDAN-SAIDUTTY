LGBTQ+ Flags and Their Meanings

Rainbow Flag

Represents the diversity of the LGBTQ+ community. Originally designed with 8 colors, now commonly displayed with 6.

Transgender Flag

Designed by Monica Helms in 1999. Blue for boys, pink for girls, and white for those transitioning or non-binary.

Progress Pride Flag

Adds arrow-shaped stripes representing transgender people and LGBTQ+ people of color to the traditional rainbow flag.

Bisexual Flag

Pink represents attraction to the same gender, blue to different genders, and purple represents attraction to both.

Pansexual Flag

Pink represents attraction to women, yellow to non-binary people, and blue to men.

Non-Binary Flag

Yellow for those outside the gender binary, white for many genders, purple for those with mixed genders, black for agender.

Asexual Flag

Black for asexuality, gray for gray-asexuality and demisexuality, white for non-asexual partners and allies, purple for community.

Lesbian Flag

Represents the lesbian community with shades from dark orange to pink, with white in the center.

Genderqueer Flag

Lavender for androgyny, white for agender identities, and green for those outside the binary.

Intersex Flag

Yellow and purple circle symbolize wholeness and completeness, and the right to be whoever and how we want to be.

THE UPCOMING LGBTQ LEADER

SFVAN GAY is emerging as a powerful voice and leader for the LGBTQ+ community in Wayanad and beyond. As the first openly transgender individual from the region, their journey represents hope, resilience, and the transformative power of living authentically.

Through advocacy, community organizing, and personal example, SFVAN GAY is breaking down barriers and creating a more inclusive society where everyone can live with dignity and respect, regardless of their gender identity or sexual orientation.

"My mission is to create a world where no one has to hide who they are or whom they love. Authenticity is our greatest strength."

As an upcoming leader, SFVAN GAY focuses on education, healthcare access, legal rights, and creating safe spaces for LGBTQ+ individuals in Wayanad and throughout Kerala.

Transgender & Gay History

Ancient Recognition

Many ancient civilizations including India, Greece, and Native American cultures recognized third genders and same-sex relationships as natural variations of human experience.

Modern Movement

The modern LGBTQ+ rights movement began with events like the Stonewall riots in 1969, where transgender women of color were at the forefront of the struggle for equality.

Legal Progress

Recent decades have seen significant legal victories including marriage equality, anti-discrimination laws, and legal recognition of transgender identities in many countries.

Ongoing Challenges

Despite progress, LGBTQ+ individuals still face violence, discrimination, and legal challenges in many parts of the world, highlighting the continued need for advocacy and education.
